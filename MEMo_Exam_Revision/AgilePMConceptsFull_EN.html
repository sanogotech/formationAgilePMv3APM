1. Introduction to AgilePM and DSDM

AgilePM is a sophisticated framework for Agile Project Management, built upon the **Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM)**. It aims to bridge the gap between high-level corporate governance and rapid, iterative product development. It focuses on delivering the right solution at the right time through disciplined collaboration.

The DSDM Philosophy

The DSDM philosophy is **'The best business value emerges when projects are aligned to clear business goals, frequently deliver reliable products, and involve collaboration from empowered teams.'** AgilePM is DSDM-compliant and provides a structured, enterprise-level approach, emphasizing fitness for business purpose and active stakeholder involvement.

2. Key Concepts, Principles, and Variables

The core of AgilePM lies in its approach to project variables and its commitment to the **8 Principles**. Unlike traditional methods where scope is fixed, AgilePM fixes Time, Cost, and Quality, using scope (features) as the primary variable to absorb pressure.

The 8 Principles (Non-Negotiable)

These principles are the non-negotiable rules for any project wishing to be DSDM compliant. They guide every decision and activity in the project lifecycle. Failure to follow even one principle compromises the entire approach.

  1. Focus on the Business Need
  2. Deliver on Time
  3. Collaborate
  4. Never Compromise Quality
  5. Build Incrementally from Firm Foundations
  6. Develop Iteratively
  7. Communicate Continuously and Clearly
  8. Demonstrate Control

3. Scrum and AgilePM: Comparison and Integration

While often confused, AgilePM (DSDM) and Scrum serve different purposes. AgilePM is a robust **Project Management** framework covering the entire lifecycle from concept to delivery and benefits realization. Scrum is an **Empirical Process Control** framework focused on product development (the 'doing').

Integration Opportunities

AgilePM can incorporate Scrum-like practices, particularly within its **Evolutionary Development (Evo Dev)** phase. The daily stand-up, iterative delivery, and focus on a small, cross-functional team are common ground. However, AgilePM provides the governance wrapper (Sponsor, Project Manager, Business Case, etc.) that Scrum often assumes is handled externally.

4. AgilePM Fundamentals: The Four Pillars

AgilePM success hinges on the alignment of four fundamental factors: **Principles, People, Process, and Products**. These four pillars ensure that a project is not just using Agile techniques, but is truly DSDM compliant and capable of delivering value within fixed constraints.

Interdependence of Fundamentals

The Principles provide the mindset, the People execute the work within the defined Process, and the Products ensure governance and control. For instance, the 'Collaborate' Principle (People) is enabled by the Facilitated Workshop technique (Process) and results in agreed requirements (Product).

5. Individuals and Interactions: The AgilePM Roles

AgilePM specifies 13 defined roles to ensure clear lines of accountability and communication across the project and business. These roles are grouped into three primary categories: **Business**, **Management**, and **Technical**. All key stakeholders, especially those in Business and Management, must be actively involved.

The Three Role Categories

The **Business** roles define the 'what' and 'why' (e.g., Sponsor, Visionary, Ambassador). The **Management** roles oversee the 'how' and 'when' (e.g., Project Manager). The **Technical** roles focus on the delivery and quality of the solution (e.g., Team Leader, Developers).

6. Collaboration and Communication (Principles 3 & 7)

The principles of **Collaborate** and **Communicate Continuously and Clearly** underpin the AgilePM approach. Active, continuous involvement from all stakeholders (especially the Business Ambassador) is key. The most effective method for requirements gathering and decision-making is the **Facilitated Workshop**.

Techniques for Effective Communication

AgilePM prefers **rich communication** (face-to-face) over lean communication (written documents) whenever possible to prevent misunderstandings and accelerate decision-making. Techniques like **Modeling** (UML, wireframes, prototypes) are used to clarify requirements visually, and **Iterative Development** itself acts as a communication mechanism by providing a tangible product for feedback.

7. AgilePM Process Framework and the Project Lifecycle

The AgilePM lifecycle is a structured, six-phase model designed to provide necessary governance and control without losing the benefits of Agile iteration. This process ensures the project remains aligned with the business need from start to finish and delivers tangible business value.

The Six Phases

The phases are Pre-Project (Initiation), Feasibility (Viability Check), Foundations (Detailed Planning and Firm Foundations), Evolutionary Development (Iterative Build and Review), Deployment (Solution Delivery to Business), and Post-Project (Benefits Realization). The Evo Dev phase is the core of the project where Timeboxes occur, followed by the critical Deployment phase.

8. Defining and Estimating the Work of the Project

Requirements are captured in the **Prioritised Requirements List (PRL)**. They are written in an appropriate format (e.g., User Stories or Features) and must be clear, testable, and prioritized using MoSCoW. Estimation is an ongoing activity that becomes more accurate as the project progresses.

Estimation Techniques

AgilePM prefers relative sizing (e.g., Story Points, T-shirt sizes) over absolute days/hours for requirements. The estimate represents the **effort required**, allowing the Team Leader to determine what fits into the fixed Timebox duration. The team performing the work must be involved in the estimation.

9. MoSCoW Prioritization

MoSCoW is the essential prioritization technique used in AgilePM to manage and flex scope while ensuring the **Deliver on Time** principle is maintained. It ensures focus on the most critical features first.

The Categories and Allocation

The total effort of all requirements must be considered against the available capacity of the team. A typical rule of thumb is that **Must Haves** should account for no more than 60% of the effort, allowing time for testing, quality, and the inclusion of Should Haves and Could Haves which provide contingency.

10. Planning and Controlling the Work of the Project

Planning in AgilePM occurs at three distinct levels, each corresponding to a different product: **Project (Project Plan)**, **Phase (Delivery Plan)**, and **Timebox (Timebox Plan)**. Control is achieved by measuring progress against these plans and upholding the fixed Time, Cost, and Quality parameters.

Control Mechanisms

The principle of **Demonstrate Control** is vital. Control is maintained through daily monitoring (Daily Stand-up), regular reporting (Project Status Report), and formal reviews (Timebox Review and Project Review). If a Timebox is at risk, the immediate action is to **flex scope (drop 'C' or 'S' requirements)**, not extend time.

11. Risk Management and the Project Approach

Risk management is proactive in AgilePM, starting early in the Feasibility phase. The **Project Approach Questionnaire (PAQ)** is a formal tool used to assess the environment, stakeholders, and technology to determine the optimal deployment of AgilePM for the specific project context.

PAQ Factors

The PAQ assesses project factors such as **Team Capability**, **Stakeholder Commitment**, and **Environmental Stability**. High scores indicate a high-risk environment but also a high potential for success with a structured Agile approach. Risk is captured in the **Risk Log** and reviewed at the Project, Phase, and Timebox levels.

12. Governance, Compliance, and the AgilePM Products

Governance in AgilePM is the structure that ensures the project operates within organizational standards and remains aligned with strategic goals. This structure is primarily provided by the formal **Products** (documentation) created throughout the lifecycle.

The Three Product Categories

Products fall into three categories: **Management** (how the project is run), **Requirements** (what the solution must do), and **Solution** (the delivered system and its deployment). Compliance ensures the project adheres to both the 8 Principles and the organizational PM standards.
